Moreover, articles were also researched to gain more knowledge on the next step of the project: determining whether eavesdroppers are present in the system and are reading the transmitted information.

Article 1:

Quantum Secret Sharing with Polarization-Entangled Photon Pairs

Key Take-Aways:

  • Quantum Secret Sharing (QSS) is an enhanced feature of Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)
  • QSS involves multiple parties that each have a portion of the information needed to create the key; without all the parties, the key cannot be used.
  • Uses Quantum Bit Error Rates (QBER) to assess security of the system. QSS typically had QBERs of 4.5% - 6.5%, which is the amount of information an intercepter would know. This is relatively insignificant because very little of the key will be revealed to the interceptor.
  • QSS has accuracy rates of about 90%.
  • Drawback: All parties must be able to transmit information, which could cause the system to be slow. Moreover, the use of more parties requires more resources, which may not be feasible if this cryptosystem is applied to the general world population.

Article 2:

High Error-Rate Quantum Key Distribution for Long-Distance Communication

Key Take-Aways

  • In typical quantum systems, an eavesdropper introduces a QBER of at least 25% into the communication channel.
  • The more parties introduced into the transmission of the information, the lower the QBER becomes when a eavesdropper is present in the system.
  • The researchers developed a new, more efficient way of determining whether eavesdroppers are present in the system: the Index Transmission Error Rate (ITER).
  • The more parties introduced into the transmission of the information, the higher the ITER becomes when a eavesdropper is present in the system. Moreover, it is easier to determine how many parties have been impacted by the eavesdropping interference because the ITER values vary more greatly, by about 5%-10%.
  • This article also shows the derivation of the QBER and ITER formulas.
